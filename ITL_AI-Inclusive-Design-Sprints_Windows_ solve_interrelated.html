Context
The power dialog appears, and the user must choose among options whose consequences differ enormously: shut down versus restart versus sleep — one of which will end their session and, depending on their setup, their ability to summon help until someone else intervenes.
OS Mismatch

Speech & Communication
Shared Limitation
All three cannot produce speech on demand: selective mutism makes speaking situationally impossible regardless of intent, social anxiety can make performative speech to a machine acutely distressing, and nonverbal autistic users may not use speech at all. “Just say it” is precisely the instruction that fails.
Context
The user is configuring hands-free features — quite possibly setting them up for the situations where speaking is hard — and the setup flow reaches its verification step.
OS Mismatch
The setup flow requires a spoken confirmation to proceed: a voice sample, a spoken phrase, an audible “yes” — with no typed, clicked, or switch-based equivalent offered at that step. The gate to the accessibility feature is itself inaccessible to part of its audience, and the flow's silent assumption — that everyone configuring voice features can comfortably speak right now — filters out exactly the users with the most nuanced relationship to speech.

Sensory & Environmental
Shared Limitation
All three are taxed by ambient motion: SPD systems must actively process every moving element in view, autistic users spend real effort filtering visual noise to reach the signal, and ADHD attention is involuntarily captured by movement. A screen where things move is a screen that never stops making demands.
Context
It's a fresh install — first sign-in on a new or reset machine — the moment the OS presents its defaults and, implicitly, its assumptions about what people want.
OS Mismatch
First sign-in lands with lock-screen motion, animated widgets, and interface effects all on, and setup includes no sensory step: dozens of choices about accounts and telemetry, none about motion, sound, or stimulation level. The user must first endure the maximal sensory configuration, then hunt down each animation source individually across scattered settings. Calm is achievable — but only as an expert task, never as an offered starting point.

Sensory & Environmental
Shared Limitation
All three pay a cost for visual noise behind text: SPD and autistic users must actively suppress the background to read the foreground, and shimmering, semi-visible movement is a classic migraine aggravator. Reading through interference is possible for them — but it converts every menu into work.
Context

Transparency can be disabled — but as one more setting to discover, not a question ever asked — and until it is found, the user reads all day through a fog the OS added on purpose.

Category
Speech & Communication
Shared Limitation
All three speak with irregular timing as a matter of course: stuttering produces blocks and pauses within words, aphasia inserts word-finding gaps mid-sentence, and dysarthria slows articulation throughout. Pauses are not the end of their speech — they are part of it.
Context
The user dictates a document, working through their thoughts at their natural cadence, which includes silences that a typical-speech model would read as completion.
OS Mismatch
System dictation stops listening after brief pauses: the timeout is tuned to fluent speech, so mid-thought silence is treated as “done” and the microphone closes. The user resumes speaking into a dead session, loses the sentence, and must notice the failure, reactivate dictation, and reconstruct their place — again and again. The tool most valuable to people who struggle with typing is calibrated to reject the speech patterns of people who struggle with speaking.

Traumatic Brain Injury → ADHD → Dementia
Category
Cognitive & Memory
Shared Limitation
All three struggle with invisible state boundaries: understanding that “my stuff is still open somewhere I can't see it” requires holding a model of hidden sessions that TBI, ADHD, and dementia each undermine differently. If the consequences of a choice aren't stated at the moment of choosing, they effectively don't exist.
Context
A family shares one PC. The user is done for now, someone else needs the machine, and they face the account menu's options for stepping away.
OS Mismatch
“Switch user” and “sign out” sit side by side with their radically different consequences unexplained: one preserves the session invisibly, the other closes everything — and unsaved work is lost by whoever guesses wrong, sometimes discovered only when the first user returns hours later to find their documents gone. The menu treats a destructive choice and a safe one as interchangeable labels, on the shared machines where the least technical family members are most likely to be the ones choosing.

Vestibular Disorder → Motion Sensitivity → Migraine
Category
Sensory & Environmental
Shared Limitation
For all three, full-field visual motion is read by the brain as self-motion: the vestibular system reports stillness while the eyes report the world sliding sideways, and that conflict produces genuine vertigo, nausea, or migraine onset — a physiological event, not a preference violation.
Context
The user switches virtual desktops — a core organizational feature they may rely on heavily for exactly the focus and simplification their condition rewards.
OS Mismatch
The full-screen slide animation plays with reduce-motion off by default: the entire visual field sweeps sideways on every switch, the single most vestibularly provocative motion pattern the OS ships, enabled out of the box. The setting that would calm it exists but is opt-out rather than opt-in, so every affected user must experience the trigger first, correctly attribute their symptoms to it, and then locate the fix — a diagnostic journey the default forced on them.

Shared Limitation
All three experience zoom and scale motion as depth motion: elements that grow and shrink read to the brain as looming toward or receding from the viewer, a depth illusion that vestibular systems, Ménière's episodes, and concussed motion processing each convert into swimming disorientation or nausea.

Shared Limitation
All three are destabilized by sudden unexpected visual events: an abrupt full-screen change forces a re-orientation their motion processing performs badly, and unpredictability compounds it — motion that can't be braced for hits harder than motion that can.

OS Mismatch
