Session 2: AAC as Student Voice: Communication, Identity, and Belonging in Schools

Date: Friday, March 27, 2026 | 12:30–1:51 PM

Summary

This session was a candid, practitioner-led conversation about AAC in K–12 school settings, focusing on where Microsoft and Windows can better support AAC users, the role of AI in AAC, district IT barriers, symbol systems, and the future of communication technology. Bryce framed it as a listening session — not a sales pitch — to understand real challenges and identify where Microsoft can meaningfully contribute.

Thematic Breakdown

  • Districts like Bellevue are Microsoft 1:1 device districts but consistently maintain ~1% iPads for AAC — a systemic tension Bryce named directly with leadership.
  • Ali Havanec identified a critical unmet need: tier 1 and tier 2 AAC support on Windows devices, so all students — not just those qualifying for special ed — can access communication tools. She cited FlexSpeak as an example of a tool she'd love to see Microsoft acquire or integrate.
  • Bryce pointed to Cboard (Windows Store, free tier) as an existing option, though participants noted it is no longer fully free and has limitations.
  • Microsoft currently funds Cboard's AI development via Azure AI credits.
  • Leslie Rytel highlighted the value of embedded symbol support across Microsoft products (Word, Teams, captions) — not just a standalone AAC app — as a high-impact, lower-barrier entry point.
  • Boardmaker symbols are currently licensed within Immersive Reader only; broader integration was flagged as desirable.

  • Taylor Phu described using Teams during COVID as the primary bridge for AAC users to communicate with therapists — but noted that AAC devices need to remain dedicated to communication, separate from the academic device.
  • Desire for an AAC app that floats as a persistent overlay on Windows (like an on-screen keyboard) so students can work and communicate simultaneously.
  • Leslie noted AAC users struggle in Teams calls because communication partners can't see when they're composing — requesting a visual "typing indicator" for AAC users in Teams.

  • SymbolStix and Boardmaker are preferred largely because they cross multiple AAC apps, giving students consistency across devices and settings.
  • Ali (citing Janice Light's research at Penn State) noted that symbol guessability is often poor — kids frequently can't identify what a symbol means without training. Consistency of use is what makes a symbol system valuable, not inherent quality.
  • Heidi cited Karen Erickson and David Koppenhaver: symbols used for reading/writing slow down literacy development — letters are the more evidence-based support for reading/writing. Symbols are most appropriate for supporting fast, efficient communication.
  • Sara Ayars added: some literate autistic adults still prefer symbols — preference must always be honored.
  • Global Symbols was highlighted as an AI-integrated symbol set enabling culturally customized symbol generation via prompts.
  • Cultural representation in symbols remains a gap — skin tones and cultural contexts are often inaccurate or non-representative.

  • Sara Ayars returned from ATIA excited about AI in AAC but found it was a non-starter for district IT, even for locally-stored AI.
  • Ali described a months-long effort to get Grid 3 approved in Bellevue SD — blocked specifically because of OpenAI integration and offshore data storage. Districts have approved Copilot, Colleague AI, and Magic School AI, but are very selective.
  • Chris Gibbons (Smartbox/Grid) shared that Grid has an AI ethics policy and an opt-in toggle — but districts don't trust user-level toggles; they want district-level MDM control (e.g., via Jamf).
  • Leslie suggested: if AI were embedded in a district-approved tool (e.g., Colleague AI), the approval path might be smoother.
  • Bryce noted that much of Microsoft's Windows AI is local/on-device, not cloud-based — Surface Pro can run GPT-4-class models locally — and suggested this could ease district privacy concerns significantly.
  • Allison raised the need for communication partner training alongside AI tools — especially for paraprofessionals — to ensure authentic authorship for AAC users.

  • Kate Glazko (UW PhD, HCI): Building an agentic AI tool that validates AAC symbol accuracy and braille output using a university-controlled, locally-hosted AI model — potential for a Copilot partnership with university oversight.
  • Tobias Weinberg (Cornell PhD, AAC user): Paper — "Why So Serious? Exploring Timely Humorous Comments in AAC through AI-powered Interfaces" — live demo of AI-powered real-time humor generation for AAC users. Website: TOBIWG.com.
  • Jutta Treviranus (OCAD University): Working on AAC built from Amazon Fire tablets using local AI + Bliss Symbolics — exploring nonlinear, non-English language models for low-cost global AAC.
  • Alexander Liddell: Building AI-assisted AAC using Llama + Eleven Labs + MIDI controllers to minimize cost.
  • Magic School AI (Heidi Brislin): Used with AAC users for authentic writing — partner-assisted scanning for choice-making, built-in scaffolding, and a student-side tutor for curriculum support (e.g., Romeo and Juliet at appropriate reading level).
  • Goblin Tools: Referenced by Bryce as a comparable AI scaffold tool.

  • Bryce raised a philosophical question: if AI can translate any language in real-time, does a student need to learn English? Conversation touched on the tension between communication empowerment and shared societal experience.
  • Bliss Symbolics highlighted as a nonlinear, non-English symbol system that pairs well with small local AI models — created by a 12-year-old girl (Rachel Zimmerman Brachman), shared as a Women's History Month fact by Sarah Kinsella.
  • Chris Gibbons: Framed the global stakes — millions of people globally live without AAC access due to geography and cost; low-cost, local AI AAC could be a "huge game changer."
