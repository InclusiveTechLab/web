Examples of potential exclusions for Mobile applications - Inclusive Tech Lab

Anywhere drag to scrub media

User drags horizontally anywhere on the player surface to move the playhead.

Perceivable

  • The progress track uses a very thin line that blends into the video poster frame; people with low vision cannot distinguish the current position from the background.
  • Chapter markers are indicated by color alone without shapes or labels; people with color-vision differences cannot perceive segment boundaries.
  • Time bubble appears beneath the finger and is occluded; users can’t see the target timestamp.

Operable

  • Fine scrubbing needs precise movement; no jump-back/forward buttons for coarse control.
  • Vertical drift cancels or jumps the seek; no 10-second step buttons for coarse control.
  • The player captures horizontal drags even when the user intended to scroll the page; people using touch exploration or screen magnification accidentally seek instead of navigating.
  • Small time steps require micro-movements; there is no way to nudge by a fixed amount using accessible buttons or keyboard focus.

Understandable

  • No explanation of drag zones; users don’t understand why taps sometimes seek and sometimes pause.
  • Chapters and boundaries aren’t indicated; users don’t understand why the seek jumps segments.
  • The player seeks on any drag but pauses on a tap; the lack of an initial tutorial causes confusion about when playback will stop or move.
  • After seeking, the player resumes automatically without announcing the new time; users cannot confirm that the intended moment was selected.

Augmented Reality plane detection and placement

User moves device to detect a surface and place an object.

Perceivable

  • Subtle dot grids/lines on bright floors aren’t perceivable under glare.
  • Surface indicators are low contrast on glossy floors; placement cues are missed.
  • Plane visualization is only a subtle shimmer that disappears against patterned carpets; users cannot perceive where objects can be placed.
  • Ambient lighting changes cause tracking dots to flicker; the moment of successful plane detection is not clearly visible.

Operable

  • The camera requires continuous sweeping at shoulder height; there is no seated or tabletop scan mode for people with limited mobility.
  • Continuous motion scan is required; there’s no static tap-to-place option for limited mobility.
  • Device tilt beyond a narrow range causes detection to reset; users with tremor cannot maintain the required pose to complete placement.

Understandable

  • Instructions use AR jargon (“detect a plane”); users don’t understand the task.
  • Copy references planes anchors and meshes; users don’t understand Augmented Reality terminology.
  • The application does not explain how to “scan” in plain language (for example, “slowly move the camera side to side across the floor”); users cannot form a correct mental model of the task.
  • Placement snapping jumps the object to a different surface without explanation; users misinterpret whether they placed the object on the floor or a wall.

Bottom sheet drag to expand/collapse

User drags a handle to change a bottom sheet’s height.

Perceivable

  • Handle has low contrast and small size; hard to see against content.
  • Handle and scrim merge with underlying content; state changes are hard to see.
  • The handle icon lacks a label for screen readers; blind users do not hear that a “Drag to expand or collapse” control exists.
  • The scrim opacity change is subtle; users cannot perceive that content behind the sheet is inactive.

Operable

  • Drag-only interaction excludes keyboard/switch/voice; no tap-to-toggle control.
  • Sheet demands precise drag angles; tap or button alternatives are missing for switch and keyboard users.
  • The sheet ignores keyboard focus and cannot be expanded with arrow keys or a button; keyboard-only users are blocked.
  • The drag gesture conflicts with system back navigation on the left edge; users accidentally exit instead of resizing.

Understandable

  • Sheet states (peek/half/full) aren’t named; users can’t predict what each state contains.
  • States have no labels like Peek Half Full; users can’t form a mental model of levels.
  • Newly revealed actions appear outside the viewport with no auto-scroll; users do not realize more options exist after expansion.
  • The sheet snaps to intermediate heights without naming them (for example, “Half”); users cannot predict where content will land after release.

Camera QR / barcode scanning

User points the camera at a code to scan.

Perceivable

  • Aim box and success feedback are too subtle; users can’t perceive alignment or success.
  • Ambient-success cues are subtle; scan confirmation isn’t visible in bright outdoor light.
  • The success chime is the only confirmation; people who are deaf or hard of hearing do not perceive that the code was read.
  • The alignment reticle is ultra-thin and disappears in bright outdoor glare; the framing target cannot be seen.

Operable

  • Requires steady hands and close focus; no manual input option present.
  • Auto-focus demands steady hands; no manual shutter or code entry alternative exists.
  • Continuous autofocus pumps the image; users with hand tremor cannot hold steady long enough to complete the scan.
  • Auto-capture fires immediately when a code enters the frame; there is no “manual shutter” to allow users time to stabilize.

Understandable

  • No instruction about distance/lighting; users don’t know how to improve recognition.
  • No guidance on framing angle or distance; users don’t understand how to improve recognition.
  • When scanning fails, the application merely says “Try again” without actionable guidance (for example, “move closer” or “increase lighting”).
  • The application does not state which code types are supported (for example, Data Matrix versus quick-response code); users retry with incompatible codes.

Chat composer expand with keyboard

User taps the composer; it expands and shifts the thread as the keyboard appears.

Perceivable

  • Composer placeholder and controls get occluded by the keyboard; users can’t see what they type.
  • The latest message is covered by the keyboard; auto-scroll doesn’t keep the conversation tail visible.
  • When the keyboard appears, the input caret is scrolled off-screen; users cannot see where text is being entered.
  • The send state is conveyed by color only (for example, disabled gray); users with color-vision differences cannot tell when sending is available.

Operable

  • Send/attach buttons move during expansion; users with motor limits miss them mid-motion.
  • Send/attach buttons move as suggestions appear; users with motor limits miss moving targets.
  • The composer steals focus each time a suggestion chip appears; switch users lose their place in the thread.
  • The input loses focus on device rotation; users must re-activate the field to continue typing.

Understandable

  • No cues about attachment limits or enter-to-send; users misunderstand how to submit.
  • Enter-to-send vs newline behavior is unstated; users can’t predict whether Return will send.
  • The application silently switches between “Enter to send” and “Enter for newline” based on context; users accidentally send unfinished messages.
  • Attachment limits (file size or count) are not stated until after failure; users do not know why the action was rejected.

Contextual keyboard type selection

App switches the on-screen keyboard to match expected input (e.g., number/email).

Perceivable

  • Keyboard change is silent; users don’t perceive why keys moved or symbols vanished.
  • Numeric layout hides needed characters (e.g., + or .); users don’t perceive where to find them.
  • When switching to the numeric layout, the hint text remains “email”; the mismatch is not perceivable at a glance.
  • Alternate characters (such as plus sign or period) are hidden behind long press without visual hint; users cannot discover essential keys.

Operable

  • No alternative input affordance when required key isn’t present; users can’t complete input.
  • Keyboard switch clears partial input or fights autocorrect; no paste/format assist is offered.
  • The keyboard switches layouts mid-entry and moves keys; users relying on motor memory repeatedly hit the wrong key.
  • There is no hardware-keyboard path for users with physical keyboards; the application traps focus in the on-screen keyboard.

Understandable

  • No hint about accepted formats; users don’t understand why validation fails.
  • Accepted formats (e.g., phone with country code) aren’t shown; users misread validation rules.
  • The input accepts multiple formats but does not display an example; users guess at the required structure and get validation errors.
  • The application masks the input (for example, password) without a “show” option; users are unsure what they typed.

Operable

  • Two-handed hold plus tap is required; no voice shutter or volume-key control alternative.
  • The only shutter option is an on-screen button placed far from reach; there is no hardware volume-key shutter or voice command.
  • Auto-crop requires holding the device parallel to the document; people with limited dexterity cannot achieve the necessary angle.

Understandable

  • No coaching overlays about glare perspective or background; users don’t know why capture fails.

Operable

  • List doesn’t auto-scroll while dragging; users can’t move items beyond the current viewport.
  • There is no keyboard path (for example, move up or move down); users who rely on keyboard or switch access cannot reorder.
  • The list does not auto-scroll when dragging near edges; items cannot be moved beyond the current viewport.

Understandable

  • No preview of destination or numbering; users don’t understand resulting order.
  • Numbers renormalize after drop without highlight; users can’t confirm the final order rules.
  • The application does not indicate whether the new order will be saved automatically; users are unsure if a “Save” action is required.
  • Dragging across collapsed groups changes hierarchy without warning; users do not understand why items moved categories.

Drag to rate or react slider

User drags a thumb on a slider to rate content or pick a reaction.

Perceivable

  • Slider track color alone indicates selection; users can’t perceive exact value.
  • Value markers appear only after release; users can’t perceive interim values while dragging.
  • Only color intensity indicates value; there is no numeric label or tick marks to perceive the selected level.
  • The reaction icons have minimal contrast and are very small; users cannot distinguish options.

Operable

  • Small thumb and narrow track require fine motor control; no stepper or presets.
  • Precise placement is required to hit discrete values; no tap-to-select or stepper controls.
  • The slider snaps to tiny steps that are hard to target; there is no plus or minus control to adjust one unit at a time.
  • The thumb requires a precise press to grab; screen-reader users cannot adjust the value with standard gestures.

Understandable

  • Scale meaning (1–5; low–high) isn’t explained; users can’t interpret outcomes.
  • Scale direction reverses in RTL without notice; users misinterpret low vs high.
  • The scale meaning (for example, “1 is negative, 5 is positive”) is not explained; users misinterpret what higher values mean.
  • The application does not explain whether ratings are public or private; users hesitate to choose.

Freehand drawing canvas with pressure

User draws with finger/stylus; pressure/velocity affect stroke.

Perceivable

  • Cursor/brush preview is subtle; users can’t perceive active brush size or color.
  • Brush preview has low contrast against the canvas; users can’t perceive active tool or color.
  • The active tool state (for example, brush, eraser, or selection) is indicated only by a subtle color ring; users cannot perceive which tool is active.
  • The grid or page background reduces contrast with the stroke color; users cannot see fine details at common zoom levels.

Operable

  • Palm rejection and small handles complicate control; no undo/redo buttons near thumb reach.
  • Two-finger pan conflicts with system gestures; no dedicated pan/zoom mode or on-screen toggles.
  • Two-finger pan conflicts with device-level gestures; users inadvertently switch applications while drawing.
  • Small controls (for example, color pickers) are placed outside thumb reach; there is no alternative toolbar layout.

Understandable

  • Tool semantics (brush vs eraser vs smudge) aren’t introduced; users misapply tools.
  • Layers are implicit; strokes appear ‘missing’ when drawn on hidden layers; users don’t understand why.
  • The effect of pressure versus speed on stroke width is not explained; users cannot predict how marks will appear.
  • Layer order and visibility are not surfaced; users think strokes “disappear” without understanding that another layer is on top.

Haptic feedback cues

User relies on vibration patterns to confirm actions.

Perceivable

  • No visual/text equivalent for haptics; deaf-blind or muted-haptics users miss feedback.
  • Vibration patterns run during silent mode with no on-screen mirror; feedback is missed.
  • Vibration patterns have no on-screen mirrors (for example, text or icons); users who have haptics disabled miss critical confirmation.
  • The device’s Do Not Disturb mode suppresses vibrations; the application does not provide a visual fallback.

Operable

  • Haptics too strong or weak; no setting to disable or amplify feedback.
  • Haptics cannot be tuned or disabled in-app; users with hypersensitivity can’t operate comfortably.
  • There is no preference to reduce intensity or disable patterns in-app; users with tactile hypersensitivity cannot use the feature comfortably.
  • Haptic events fire during long presses and interfere with dwell-based input; users release prematurely.

Understandable

  • Meaning of patterns isn’t explained; users can’t learn success vs error vibrations.
  • Meaning of patterns is undocumented; users can’t map a vibration to an outcome.
  • The meaning of different patterns (success, warning, error) is undocumented; users cannot learn the vocabulary.
  • Haptic patterns differ across platforms without explanation; users cannot transfer knowledge between devices.
